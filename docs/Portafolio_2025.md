# Personal Statement Summary

I hold a B.Sc. in Electronic Engineering from Universidad Distrital
(2009), an M.Sc. in Systems and Computing Engineering from Universidad
Nacional de Colombia (2013), a second M.Sc. in Systems and Computing
Engineering from Universidad de los Andes (2019), and a Ph.D. in
Engineering from Universidad de los Andes (2019).

Following my undergraduate studies, I worked for three years as a
Software Development Engineer and subsequently for one year as IT
Department Coordinator at the Center for Research for Development (CID),
an institution dedicated to applied research, advisory and consulting
services, as well as the oversight and auditing of public contracts.
During this period, I participated in large-scale software development
projects for both government institutions and the private sector,
gaining substantial experience in applied engineering and project
management.

In parallel, I maintained a strong commitment to teaching, serving as a
lecturer at Universidad Nacional de Colombia and later at Universidad
Sergio Arboleda. I began my master's studies in 2011 thanks to a
teaching assistantship scholarship from Universidad Nacional. My
master's thesis, "Fault Detection and Diagnosis for Wind Turbines Using
a Data Mining Approach" (2013), was among the early works in Colombia to
apply machine learning models to fault detection in renewable energy
systems.

Motivated by national government scholarship opportunities and a growing
interest in research, I began my Ph.D. in 2015 at Universidad de los
Andes. My doctoral research focused on Semantic Web technologies and
knowledge graphs for identifying prerequisite relationships between
concepts, under the supervision of Dr. Olga Mariño. My doctoral
experience was highly successful and ultimately decisive in my
commitment to pursuing an academic career.

I completed my Ph.D. in 2019 and subsequently worked for nearly two
years as an Artificial Intelligence Engineer at Reconoser ID, where I
led the development of facial recognition systems and face- and
voice-based anti-spoofing technologies. The products I designed and
developed were adopted by multiple fintech companies, which integrated
the anti-spoofing systems into their customer onboarding processes. In
August 2021, I joined Universidad de los Andes as an Assistant
Professor, and by the end of 2025, completed nine academic semesters at
the University.

# Executive Summary

This document presents the main outcomes of my work as a faculty member
in the Department of Systems Engineering at Universidad de los Andes,
covering the primary areas of academic work including teaching,
research, and institutional development (the "three dimensions").

My research line has been in Natural Language Processing (NLP). I was
the first faculty member to establish this research line and to develop
dedicated courses in this area. All my efforts across the three
evaluated dimensions have been focused on developing and consolidating
this line within the University. I am a recognized institutional and
national reference in NLP, and my current efforts are also directed
toward achieving international recognition. In the 2024 triennial
evaluation, the Department highlighted my efforts and achievements, as
well as the coherence of my work and its contributions to the
University. I received an outstanding evaluation across all dimensions.

**Teaching**

Over nine semesters as an Assistant Professor, I have taught 12
different courses, 4 at the undergraduate level and 8 at the master's
level. Among these, I have designed and created 4 new master's courses
in NLP that were not previously offered at Uniandes and redesigned 2
additional master's courses in Artificial Intelligence. At the
undergraduate level, I have contributed to the updating and redesign of
two of the courses I taught.

In total, I have served 1,843 student enrollments, achieving an average
course evaluation score of 4.6/5 (158 points), placing me above the
University average.

In continuing education (a.k.a. EDCO), I have designed and coordinated 4
courses in NLP and IA offered on multiple occasions, enrolling 310
students and generating COP 432,647,530 in revenue. I have also served
as an instructor for 12 additional continuing education courses, with
1,694 students enrolled and approximately COP 1,761,873,200 in revenue.

Furthermore, I was the first Colombian Ambassador of Nvidia's Deep
Learning Institute, which has allowed me to deliver over 19 workshops to
1,188 Uniandes students, of whom 960 earned certification, representing
an estimated value of USD 480,000.

**Research**

I established and consolidated a new research line in Natural Language
Processing (NLP) at the University and within the FlagLab research
group. I led and consolidated several major research projects focused on
NLP applications, most notably the Colombian Indigenous Languages
Translation project, which constitutes the most significant national
effort to date in the construction of parallel corpora and the
development of neural machine translation models for these languages.

In parallel, I consolidated a research program on Large Language Models
(LLM) agents in collaboration with multiple departments, focusing on the
development of cooperative and resilient systems, supported through
sponsorship and collaboration with Microsoft and Google. Additional
projects include the development of virtual patients for medical
training and AI-based systems for spoofing detection.

These initiatives have enabled the consolidation of a strong national
and international research network and joint participation in funding
applications, with a total of eleven submissions to different funding
programs. Overall, more than COP 300 million in research funding has
been secured through external entities and internal university calls.

Through the NLP research line, approximately 90 students have been
trained, including thesis students, research assistants, and
undergraduate research monitors. I have supervised 18 master's theses
and 44 undergraduate thesis projects. At the doctoral level, I
supervised one PhD student who has successfully completed the program
and I am currently supervises three doctoral students in their first or
early second year.

In terms of scholarly output, I have authored 14 journal publications in
Q1/Q2 venues and 16 papers published in top-tier AI conferences ranked
A\*/A, in addition to other publications in lower-ranked venues. Our
work has been presented at leading NLP conferences such as EMNLP and
NAACL, as well as major general AI conferences including NeurIPS and
AAAI. Despite the relatively recent establishment of the NLP research
line, the University has already gained visibility and recognition
within the international NLP community as a result of these
contributions. At the national level, I am recognized as one of the most
active researchers in NLP in Colombia and have frequently participated
as a reference speaker in national forums and panel discussions on the
topic.

I have collaborated with 12 faculty members within my department on
research projects, with 7 faculty members from other departments or
faculties within the University, and with 9 faculty members from
external universities, including 8 international collaborators.

**Institutional development**

My contributions to institutional development at Universidad de los
Andes have focused on academic governance, strategic partnerships,
talent development, and external resource acquisition in the field of
artificial intelligence. I have actively participated in academic
committees for the Master's programs in Information Engineering (MINE)
and Artificial Intelligence (MAIA), contributing to curriculum redesign,
admissions evaluation, and the consolidation of research lines in NLP
and Semantic Web. I also took part in the design of the new
undergraduate program in Data Science, particularly shaping its
Artificial Intelligence component. In parallel, I have led the
establishment of national and international cooperation agreements with
academic and industry partners, including CENIA (Centro Nacional de
Investigación en Inteligencia Artificial Chile), SoftServe (industry),
TwinMatrix (industry), and the GCF APRENDELIBRE Foundation (industry),
enabling large-scale research collaboration, student training, and the
University's participation in high-impact regional initiatives such as
LatamGPT (https://www.latamgpt.org/).

A central institutional achievement has been the creation and
consolidation of AI Week, a large-scale event co-organized with
SoftServe, and NVIDIA, which has become a regional benchmark for AI
training and industry--academia collaboration. Across its 2024 and 2025
editions, the event engaged more than 2,200 students from 96
universities across Latin America and enabled the awarding of over 630
NVIDIA certifications, valued at more than USD 335,000. Complementing
these efforts, I have contributed to securing significant external
resources through research funding, extension programs, international
grants, and strategic partnerships, generating substantial financial
returns and visibility for the University. Additionally, I co-edited the
interdisciplinary book Artificial Intelligence: Theories, Applications,
and Future, coordinated multi-faculty collaboration, and engaged in
national media outreach, reinforcing Universidad de los Andes'
leadership and public presence in artificial intelligence.

## Undergraduate

At the undergraduate level, I have taught a total of 473 students (see
Table 1). As a member of the FlagLab research group, my main teaching
activities are aligned with the formal track of the Systems and
Computing Engineering program, particularly in *Structural Mathematics*
and *Design and Analysis of Algorithms*. However, I have also been open
to contributing to courses in the software track, including
*Introduction to Programming* and *Object-Oriented Design and
Programming* courses. Through my participation in these courses, I have
supported the department during periods of curricular change, especially
in courses undergoing initial implementations that require more
experienced teaching staff.

1.  **Structural mathematics and logic (ISIS 1104)**

I taught this course during 2021. In the first semester, the evaluation
score was 4.5/5 (156 points), and in the second semester it was 5.0/5
(166 points). Structural Mathematics and Logic is the first course in
the curriculum formal methods area and aims to develop students'
foundational skills in formal reasoning using propositional and
first-order logic, as well as in the use of mathematical structures such
as sets, relations, and functions. The course also introduces number
theory, mathematical induction, and counting, with an applied focus
tailored to the needs of computer science, particularly for program
verification and development using formal methods. Originally designed
by Professors Silvia Takahashi and Rodrigo Cardoso and later redesigned
by Professor Jorge Duitama in 2023, during the year I taught the course
I supported the development of additional practical materials and
contributed to the incorporation of gamified learning activities, as
well as updated lecture presentations aligned with the notation and
structure of Rosen's Discrete Mathematics and Its Applications (6th
Edition).

2.  **Design and Analysis of Algorithms (ISIS 1105)**

This has been my primary undergraduate course, which I have taught
continuously for nine semesters. When I began teaching the course, the
curriculum was designed to first develop the skills required for
students to evaluate problem-solving algorithms in terms of correctness
and efficiency. It then aimed to enable students to apply a range of
problem-solving techniques, including divide and conquer, dynamic
programming, and graph-based algorithms. Finally, the course sought to
introduce students to the identification of NP-hard problems.

Together with the course coordinator, professor Jorge Duitama, during
2021 and 2022 we initiated a series of curricular adjustments to better
align the course with the needs of the program. One of the most
significant changes was the relocation of formal correctness proofs to
the Structural Mathematics and Logic course (ISIS 1104), where they are
addressed as a direct application of proof techniques in computer
science. This restructuring created space to strengthen the course
content by incorporating network flow algorithms in the second part of
the course and approximation algorithms in its final section.

In addition, the course projects, midterm exams, and assignments have
been revised both in content and in grading weights, with the goal of
distributing the student workload more evenly throughout the semester
rather than concentrating it toward the end.

3.  **Object-Oriented Design and Programming (ISIS 1226)**

The course covers the principles and practice of object-oriented

10. **Advanced Models in Natural Language Processing (MAIA 4332)**

This course was designed and is currently coordinated by me. It provides
an in-depth introduction to advanced Natural Language Processing (NLP)
models based on deep learning. Over an eight-week period, students
progress from fundamental neural models, such as logistic regression and
feedforward networks, to advanced architectures including RNNs, LSTMs,
encoder--decoder models, and Transformers. The course covers core
techniques such as BPE and WordPiece tokenization, static and contextual
embeddings, and the self-attention mechanism. It also examines large
language models (LLMs), text generation through sampling methods, and
fine-tuning strategies for task-specific adaptation. Prompt engineering
for improving interaction with LLMs and ethical considerations in NLP
deployment are also addressed.

By the end of the course, students are able to apply advanced NLP
techniques to text-based problems, implement deep learning--based
language models with optimized textual representations, and understand
the principles underlying fine-tuning of pretrained models and
strategies for improving text generation. This course has been offered
since the 2024--20 academic term.

**Machine Translation for Colombian Indigenous Languages**: This project
aims to develop machine translation systems for low-resource Colombian
indigenous languages such as Wayuunaiki, Arhuaco, Inga, and Nasa, using
Transformer-based architectures and transfer learning strategies. By
creating a parallel corpus and baseline translation models, the
initiative seeks to advance the digital preservation and linguistic
revitalization of these communities, fostering their inclusion in the
technological ecosystem and contributing to the protection of Colombia's
cultural and linguistic heritage.

**Evaluating Cooperative Intelligence, Resilience, and Generalization in
LLM-Based Multi-Agent Systems:** This project aims to advance the
understanding and evaluation of cooperative behaviors in Large Language
Model (LLM)--based autonomous agents operating within multi-agent
environments. Building upon three complementary research efforts, the
project examines cooperation, resilience, and social generalization
across a range of controlled settings.

First, we investigate the cooperative capabilities of LLM-augmented
autonomous agents using the Melting Pot benchmark
(<https://github.com/google-deepmind/meltingpot>), comparing their
performance against reinforcement learning baselines and simpler
reasoning-based agents. Second, we introduce and validate the concept of
cooperative resilience, proposing quantitative metrics to assess how
multi-agent systems withstand, adapt to, and recover from environmental
disruptions and the introduction of unsustainable agent behaviors.
Third, we study the generalization abilities of LLM agents in zero-shot,
mixed-motive scenarios using the Concordia simulation environment,
focusing on their capacity to identify mutual gains, negotiate, and
cooperate with diverse partners.

The project contributes methodological tools, empirical insights, and
clear evaluation criteria to support future research on reliable,
adaptive, and socially aware multi-agent AI systems. This project is
also a joint effort involving faculty from three departments within the
School of Engineering: Systems and Computing Engineering, Electronic
Engineering (Nicanor Quijano), and Biomedical Engineering (Luis
Giraldo).

**LLM-Driven Virtual Patient System for Enhancing Clinical Communication
Skills in Medical Students:** The goal of this project is to develop and
evaluate an advanced virtual patient system powered by Large Language
Models (LLMs) to enhance clinical communication skills among medical
students. In collaboration with researchers from the University of
Luxembourg, the project seeks to create a scalable and pedagogically
robust platform capable of simulating realistic patient interactions
through adaptive reasoning, contextual memory mechanisms, and human-like
conversational dynamics.

The system will enable learners to engage in flexible, repetitive, and
asynchronous clinical encounters across a diverse set of medical
scenarios. By providing immediate, scenario-specific feedback, the
platform aims to improve students' abilities in information gathering,
empathy, and clinical decision-making. The project will also assess
usability, educational effectiveness, and perceived realism through
standardized evaluation tools and expert review. This initiative aspires
to offer a cost-effective and accessible alternative to traditional
role-playing and simulation-based training, contributing to improved
communication competence and better preparedness for real clinical
environments.

**Habla**: This project emerged from the urgent need to effectively
detect AI-generated voice forgeries, a concern explicitly raised by
professional voice actors in Colombia. While existing voice spoofing and
cloning detection systems do exist, most are developed and evaluated
primarily in English, with limited attention to Spanish and virtually
none adapted to Latin American dialects.

Addressing this gap, the project tackles the lack of linguistic
diversity in automatic speaker verification research. For the first
time, we introduced HABLA, the first voice anti-spoofing dataset for
Latin American Spanish. HABLA comprises more than 22,000 authentic and
58,000 spoofed speech samples from male and female speakers across
Argentina, Colombia, Peru, Venezuela, and Chile, generated using six
state-of-the-art speech synthesis and voice conversion methods. The
first spoofing detection models and the dataset have been openly
released to the research community (https://github.com/Ruframapi/HABLA).
The initial phase of this project was conducted in collaboration with
Dr. Bernardo Pereira from the Australia National University.

## Outcomes of the FAPA (Fund for Assistant Professors)

The project funded by the FAPA (Assistant Professors' Fund), with an
approximate budget of COP 137 million, was successfully completed in
2025 within the three-year timeframe established by the University.
Following the advice generously provided by my colleagues, and in
particular by my faculty advisor, Dr. Olga Mariño, I proposed in 2022 a
transversal research project focused on NLP that could be applied across
multiple domains.

The outcomes of this approach are reflected in the research projects
presented in Section 4.4. The primary objective of the project in 2022
was to develop enriched vector representations of text that incorporate
semantic information as well as existing grammatical and morphological
features. These representations can be applied to a wide range of
domains, including indigenous language translation, recommender systems,
and other applications such as those described in Section 4.4.

From an execution standpoint, the FAPA project experienced no delays,
and the budget execution reached nearly 100%, with only approximately
COP 2 million remaining unexecuted. The academic outputs associated with
the project significantly exceeded the originally proposed goals,
resulting in more than ten publications linked to the project. The FAPA
funding supported doctoral and master's students through semester-based
funding and covered participation in selected academic conferences.

# Personal Assessment of Achievements and Integration of Responsibilities

Overall, I consider that I have performed well across the three main
dimensions of evaluation: teaching, research, and institutional
development. I have also maintained coherence across these dimensions,
with all my contributions focused on consolidating the research line in
Natural Language Processing (NLP). This line did not exist at the
university when I began as a faculty member in 2021, and I have
gradually developed it to the point of achieving national and
international recognition in leading research spaces.

In the area of teaching, I would like to highlight that I have delivered
12 different courses, including 4 undergraduate and 8 master's courses,
and have designed and implemented 4 new master's NLP courses. I have
adapted to the evolving needs of the programs and have not been limited
to a single course. I continuously strive to innovate and improve course
content while maintaining up-to-date academic standards.

This commitment to high-quality teaching is closely linked to research
outcomes. In multiple instances, students who have taken my master's
courses in NLP leave with a clear project idea and the theoretical
foundations necessary to develop it successfully. I believe I have
effectively integrated teaching and research, and the results are
evident in the publications and projects produced by my students.

Regarding institutional development, I have successfully established a
high-impact regional event, AI Week, which serves as a platform for
attracting talent to our master's programs. Many of our master's
students have noted that they discovered the program through AI Week or
the NVIDIA workshops I organize. Additionally, I have contributed
significantly to the reform and consolidation of the Systems Engineering
program in the area of Artificial Intelligence, as well as to the
development of the new undergraduate program in Data Science. As a
member of the MINE and MAIA academic committees, I have helped ensure
that these programs meet the standards of the best universities in the
country and remain competitive at the global level.

Overall, throughout the nine semesters I have spent at the university, I
have demonstrated a strong dedication to both research and teaching. In
the 2024 \"Triennial Evaluation\" conducted by the School of
Engineering, my contributions were recognized with an outstanding
performance rating in all three measured categories: teaching, research
and institutional development.

# Projection

In the coming years, as an Associate Professor, I plan to undertake
various actions aimed at maintaining and enhancing my academic
performance. These actions are categorized into three types:
consolidation, strengthening, and exploration. The initiatives outlined
below are informed by my aspirations as a researcher and educator,
alignment with the strategic needs of my department and university, and
projections regarding the future evolution of Artificial Intelligence
(AI) and Natural Language Processing (NLP).

**Consolidation Activities**

-   I have consistently demonstrated outstanding performance in
    teaching, as evidenced by my commitment to regularly updating course
    content, employing best pedagogical practices, and attentively
    responding to feedback from both colleagues and students through
    formal course evaluations. However, I recognize the complex
    challenge posed by generational shifts and the increasing use of AI
    tools, particularly in technology-oriented undergraduate programs.
    The full impact of these changes is only beginning to be understood.
    Therefore, it will be essential in the coming years to study and
    develop strategies that promote positive intergenerational synergy,
    while responsibly and ethically integrating new AI tools into the
    curriculum.

-   The FlagLab-NLP research group, in which I am actively involved, is
    recognized as a leader within the Faculty and Department. My
    intention is to further consolidate this position by increasing
    internal visibility and promotion. An area for improvement is the
    dissemination of the group's achievements within the university, as
    many colleagues who have not previously collaborated with us remain
    unaware of our work. To address this, I propose the establishment of
    regular internal seminars and small-scale outreach events to foster
    awareness and collaboration. This could also contribute to
    strengthening and consolidating the positioning of CinfonIA as the
    first academic research center in Artificial Intelligence in Latin
    America, of which I am a member.

-   I also aim to maintain or exceed current bibliometric indicators,
    and to increase the volume of publications in high-impact journals
    (Q1-Q2) and top-tier conferences (A\*-A\*). Achieving this will
    require continued support from doctoral and master's students
    affiliated with our research group.

-   Furthermore, the IAWeek, a national event that has already made a
    significant impact, will be a focal point in my future activities.
    My goal is to consolidate its status at the regional level, ensuring
    sustained growth in terms of student and university participation,
    as well as attracting new sponsors.

**Strengthening Activities**

-   My primary goal is to enhance the international presence of the NLP
    group. While our participation in leading events within the field
    has been notable, particularly at the Latin American level, it
    remains incipient compared to groups from universities in the United
    States, China, and Europe. Securing funding for computational
    resources and doctoral students will be critical, and efforts to
    obtain such resources must be prioritized in the coming years.

-   Closely related to this objective is the need to expand my success
    in international grant applications. To date, I have secured funding
    exclusively through national calls, but given the reduction in
    research resources within Colombia, obtaining support from
    international sources has become imperative. To achieve this, I will
    seek guidance from colleagues with experience in external grant
    acquisition and pursue joint applications with international peers.

-   With my deepening expertise in NLP and AI, and the development of
    curriculum content aligned with industry standards, I am now
    positioned to author a reference textbook for teaching these
    subjects.

-   Additionally, in preparation for future administrative roles within
    the university, such as coordinating a master's program or leading
    my department, I recognize the importance of strengthening my
    socio-emotional, management, and financial skills.

**Exploration Activities**

-   I intend to gain a deeper understanding of the organizational
    structure and operational dynamics of the university, the Faculty of
    Engineering, and my department, in anticipation of possible future
    administrative positions.

-   I aspire to participate in the organization of top international
    events in the field of AI or NLP, for example, serving as a chair.

-   In terms of research, I am keen to explore robotics, particularly
    the development of world models. I believe that the future of NLP
    and AI research lies in the physical world and in advancing machine
    understanding of it.

-   I view my academic trajectory as a platform for broad societal
    service. In the coming years, I aim to dedicate significant
    exploratory effort toward initiatives that translate complex AI and
    NLP research into accessible, high-impact solutions for society.
    Whether through authoring foundational texts, scaling regional
    training programs, or forging global tech partnerships, my
    overarching goal is to ensure that the transformative power of
    Artificial Intelligence serves the public interest and drives
    sustainable progress in our country and beyond.
